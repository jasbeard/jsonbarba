I keep seeing the same pattern: AI makes coding much faster, then I slow down at product and visual decisions.

In many tasks, works like a strong engineer: it scans codebases, suggests clean abstractions, writes tests, and catches edge cases. With clear constraints, I get usable drafts in minutes.

But when the question shifts from "is this correct?" to "does this feel right?".

That is where I hold back a bit.

<br />

## <span id="the-coding-edge">[The coding edge](#the-coding-edge)</span>

It excels at pattern matching and execution. In software, that means turning intent into working code fast.

It can:

- Refactor repetitive code with consistency
- Generate boilerplate and glue code quickly
- Suggest architecture options with tradeoffs
- Accelerate debugging by narrowing possible causes
- Produce decent docs and test scaffolding

This changes team velocity, especially for solo builders.

In practice, it feels like pairing with an engineer who is fast, available, and broadly knowledgeable.

<br />

## <span id="where-it-still-feels-off">[Where it still feels off](#where-it-still-feels-off)</span>

Visual and interaction design is where I still see a gap.

Looks polished at first glance, then generic on second.

Spacing may be correct but emotionally flat.

Hierarchy exists, but not always in service of the product's priorities.

Motion is present, but can miss the brand's personality and context.

The output is usually usable. Great products, though, are not judged on usability alone.

Coherence — does this screen extend the product promise? Does the visual system reinforce the message instead of competing with it? Does interaction reduce cognitive load, or just look modern?

Those are taste questions.

<br />

## <span id="why-taste-is-harder-than-correctness">[Why taste is harder than correctness](#why-taste-is-harder-than-correctness)</span>

Code quality is measurable. Taste is contextual.

Taste lives in tradeoffs:

- Restraint over feature density
- Clarity over decoration
- Familiarity over novelty (or sometimes the reverse)

The right choice depends on audience, product stage, emotional tone, and business goal. Two designs can both be valid but not equally aligned with intent.

<br />

## <span id="usage">[Usage](#usage)</span>

I don't use it to set design direction (for now). I use it to accelerate implementation.

My flow:

- Define intent (audience, priority, and tone).
- Let AI generate structure and implementation options.
- Apply a taste filter: cut noise, tighten hierarchy, simplify interactions, align tone.
- Ship what feels native to the product, not just technically correct.

AI adds speed and options. Human judgment adds coherence.

It is good enough to stand beside strong engineers for large parts of coding work.

Product must feel intentional, someone still has to guard taste: what fits, what feels forced, and what quietly serves the user.

Correctness ships features. Taste ships products people trust.
